Flood Hazards

Floods are the most systematic kind of natural disaster and happen when a cascade of water submerges commonly dry land. Floods are frequently brought on by prolonged periods of heavy rain, rapid snowmelt, or a storm surge from a tropical cyclone or tsunami in coastal areas.

What to do Before:

  • Be alert to your surroundings.

  • If flood is frequent in your area, make sure to find a higher ground in case of emergency.

  • Prepare the basic necessities for the family.

  • Prepare the most necessary things in case of an emergency evacuation.

  • Inform the family members about the evacuation plan.

  • Move the valuable things that needs to be relocated in case of hazardous scenario.

  • This website will contain what to do during a flood and how to prevent it.

What to do During:

  • If a flood is likely in your area, you should:

  • For information, turn to the radio or television.

  • Consider the possibility of sudden floods. Move right away to higher ground if a flash flood is possible. Do not wait for instructions before moving.

  • Watch out for streams, drainage channels, canyons, and other sites that are prone to unexpected flooding. In some places, flash floods can happen with or without the usual warning signs, including rain clouds or heavy rain.

  • If you must prepare to evacuate, you should do the following:

  • Secure your house. Bring outside furnishings inside if you have the time. Place your essentials on a higher floor.

  • If directed to do so, shut off utilities at main switches or valves. Unplug any electrical devices. If you are damp or standing in water, avoid touching electrical equipment.

What to do After:

  • Do not return home until authorities have advised that it is safe to do so.

  • If the main power switch was not turned off prior to flooding, do not re-enter your home until a qualified electrician has determined it is safe to do so.

  • Use extreme caution when returning to your home after a flood.

  • Appliances that may have been flooded pose a risk of shock or fire when turned on. Do not use any appliances, heating, pressure, or sewage system until electrical components have been thoroughly cleaned, dried, and inspected by a qualified electrician.

  • The main electrical panel must be cleaned, dried, and tested by a qualified electrician to ensure that it is safe.

  • Depending on where you live, your municipal or the provincial inspection authority is responsible for the permitting process required before your electric utility can reconnect power to your home.
